Every product starts as a conviction: *this should exist.* The distance between that conviction and software people rely on every day is where most projects quietly fail — not at launch, but in the months after, when reality starts disagreeing with the plan.

Here's how we structure that journey for our clients.

![Product workshop with sticky notes and sketches](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1552664730-d307ca884978?auto=format&fit=crop&w=1400&q=80)

## Discovery is cheap; rework is not

Before any code, we spend focused time on three questions: who is this for, what job does it do for them, and how will we know it's working? A week of discovery routinely saves months of building the wrong thing. The output is a scoped v1 — small enough to ship in weeks, complete enough to test the core assumption.

## Scope is a design decision

"Everything in phase one" is how budgets die. We push hard to separate the load-bearing features from the nice-to-haves. A product that does one thing reliably beats a product that does five things badly — and it earns the user trust that funds phase two.

## Build for the second year

Codebases are read far more than they're written. We choose boring, proven technology where possible, document decisions as we make them, and structure projects so a new developer can be productive in days. When your product succeeds and needs to grow, this is the difference between compounding and rewriting.

## Iterate on evidence, not opinion

After launch, analytics and user feedback replace the roadmap debates. Small weekly releases beat quarterly big bangs: risk stays low, learning stays fast, and the product bends toward what users actually do.
